Locked iPhone

February 2015

The FBI recovered an iPhone from a terrorist after an attack in San Bernardino, California. This phone could have contained contact information for the terrorist’s accomplices, plans for future attacks, and invaluable insight into how modern terrorists operate in the digital world. But it was encrypted, locked to everyone except those with the password. The FBI did not have the password. They needed to break the encryption. People have been breaking encryption since its creation of it with the Caesar Cipher. The only issue, it has gotten better. Much better. It could take a supercomputer hundreds of years to break the encryption on this phone. The cost of breaking the encryption would not be worth it. The FBI needed to bypass the encryption using a backdoor, so the FBI went to Apple. A backdoor is a way to get around the encryption. If encryption is a massive castle fortified from all sides, a back door is a hidden access through the sewers. Apple refused. Their backdoor was a closely guarded secret. Apple had to protect its reputation as an ultra-secure platform. Bypassing all their security, any time officials came knocking on their door wasn’t going to work. The FBI fought in court, citing the All Writs Act, but Apple rose the victor. The current regulation protected Apple. And the terrorist. The FBI had vital information at their figure tips, but the law held it back. A backdoor to bypass the encryption, required by regulation, could have made all the difference, but would have that been for the better?

Instant Criminal Messaging

2018

Criminals using encryption to protect themselves from authorities is not anything new. In 2018, the FBI dismantled a Canadian-based messaging service (Phillips 2021). This secure, encrypted, messaging service aided criminals in their activities. It let them walk right beside the FBI in the digital world, without the FBI having any chance of figuring out what they were up to. This instant remote communication from anywhere in the world gave criminals superpowers. Criminals can use the infrastructure that the world has set up to wreak havoc on it. The potential anonymity given by encryption is unparalleled to any time in history.

Bank Robbery, Digitally

2020

Ransomware is more common than ever now. In 2020, the Finastra Group Holdings became a victim of a ransomware attack. 90 of 100 of the world’s largest banks were affected (Hastett, 2021, pg. 1). Finastra had to go dark. They were able to recover, but potential damage was clear. Attempted cyber-attacks against banks have increased from under 5,000 a week in February 2020 to more than 200,000 a week in April 2021 (Hastett, 2021, pg. 1). It was found that in a 2021 report, in the past 12 months, 44% of financial institutions paid the ransom to get their data back. Even with backups, only 47% were able to recover, despite 67% saying they could (Hastett, 2021, pg. 2).

Healthcare Attacks

2016

The finical industry isn’t the only industry under intense pressure from ransomware attacks. The healthcare industry has seen similar trends. In a report published by the Department of Health and Human Services in 2021, it was found that in the first 5 months, 82 different ransomware attacks occurred, 72% of which exposed patient data (Kiser 2021, pg. 65). In 2016 the Hollywood Presbyterian Medical Center was attacked. Ambulances had to be diverted to other hospitals, records couldn’t be accessed, and certain computerized scans could not be completed (Kiser 2021, pg. 71). They were left with the decision of paying the ransom or losing access to all their patient data.
